Redundancy Basics

Before following the process, understand what redundancy is, why the process matters, and where the main risks sit.

What redundancy means

Redundancy is about the job no longer needing to be done by anyone. Common reasons include restructure, lower work volumes, technology changes, relocation, closure, merger, takeover, insolvency, or bankruptcy.

What makes it genuine

The job is no longer required, consultation obligations in an award or enterprise agreement have been followed, and reasonable redeployment was not available.

Why process matters

If the process is not genuine, the employee may be able to challenge the dismissal. The biggest risks are pre-determining the outcome, failing to consult, ignoring redeployment, or using redundancy to mask performance issues.

What awards usually say

Awards contain consultation clauses. They generally require employers to notify affected employees, provide information, invite views, and consider those views before implementing major workplace change.

What agreements may say

Enterprise agreements must contain consultation terms. They may also include redundancy rules or entitlements that differ from the NES, so always check the agreement itself.

What HR must protect

Fairness, consistency, documentation, employee dignity, confidentiality, decision-maker independence, and evidence that feedback was considered before the final decision.

Redundancy Process Map

This is the meeting and decision flow students should understand before using the module lessons. The key idea: notify proposed change, consult, consider, then confirm.

1 Business case

Document why the role or number of roles is proposed to change. Confirm the job is genuinely no longer required in the future structure.

2 Pre-checks

Check award, agreement, contract, policy, selection needs, redeployment options, decision-maker, payroll data, and consultation obligations.

3 Initial meeting

Notify the employee of proposed changes and that their position is at risk. Provide written information and confirm the consultation meeting.

4 Consultation

Invite questions and feedback, discuss impacts and alternatives, review vacancies, and capture the employee's response.

5 Consider feedback

The decision-maker reviews feedback, redeployment, and alternatives. Do not treat feedback as merely “noted.”

6 Final outcome

Confirm the decision in writing, explain payments and notice, offer support, complete exit steps, and keep the process file.

Timing guide: internal materials suggest consultation is ideally 1-2 weeks where practical, but timing depends on the circumstances, award/agreement requirements, business urgency, and whether the employee raises feedback that needs proper consideration.

Who may not get redundancy pay under the NES?

Less than 12 months

Employees with less than 12 months continuous service generally do not receive NES redundancy pay.

Casual employees

Casual employees are listed by Fair Work as employees who do not get redundancy pay.

Fixed period, project, or season

Employees engaged for a specific period, task, project, or season may be excluded.

Apprentices and some trainees

Apprentices and trainees engaged only for the training arrangement may not get redundancy pay.

Serious misconduct

Employees terminated for serious misconduct do not receive redundancy pay.

Small business employer

Most small businesses with fewer than 15 employees do not pay redundancy pay under the NES, but exceptions and industry schemes can apply.

Module 01 lesson

Redundancy Foundations

This module gives you the threshold test for redundancy: the focus is the position, not the person. A redundancy process starts with an operational change that means the employer no longer requires the employee's job to be performed by anyone, or where insolvency or bankruptcy creates that outcome.

Confirmed guidance Fair Work describes genuine redundancy as requiring that the job no longer needs to be done by anyone and that consultation obligations in the relevant award or enterprise agreement have been followed.
Internal training point The Inject HR materials reinforce that redundancy should not be used as a substitute for managing performance, behaviour, or interpersonal conflict.

Teach the distinction

  • Role-based: “The business is proposing to remove or change this position because of operational requirements.”
  • Person-based risk: “This person is not performing, so we will call it redundancy.”
  • Process-based risk: “The role may be redundant, but we have not checked consultation or redeployment obligations.”

Plain-English explanation

“The safest way to think about redundancy is to remove the employee's name from the first question. Ask whether the job, as a job, is still required in the future structure. If the answer depends on the employee's conduct, attitude, or manager preference, pause and get advice.”

Practice task: redundancy or not? Classify four scenarios: a duplicated role after a merger, a manager wanting to remove a difficult employee, reduced hours after a downturn, and relocation of a role interstate. For each, write down the business reason, the process risk, and what advice you would seek before action.
Quick check

A manager says, “This person is difficult and the team would run better without them. Can we make their role redundant?” What is the safest first response?

Apply this on the job

Use “the role is proposed to be made redundant” rather than “we are making the person redundant.” That language helps you keep the process role-based and avoids implying a final decision too early.

Module 02 lesson

Planning Before Notification

This module turns the concept into a prepared process. HR should not let a manager hold the first meeting until the business reason, proposed structure, documents, consultation path, and employee support plan are ready.

Documents to prepare Proposed changes letter, meeting script, consultation invitation, note-taking template, open roles list, redundancy estimate, and draft notice of redundancy.
Checks to complete Award or enterprise agreement, employment contract, company policy, payroll data, continuous service, small business status, and any associated entities.

Planning sequence

  1. Confirm the operational reason and proposed future structure.
  2. Identify affected roles and whether selection criteria are required.
  3. Check consultation obligations in awards, agreements, contracts, and policy.
  4. Identify who the decision-maker is and how employee feedback will reach them.
  5. Prepare a current list of vacancies and possible redeployment options.
  6. Coordinate meeting logistics, confidentiality, note-taking, payroll estimates, and wellbeing support.

What to look for in an award or agreement

Do not just ask whether an employee is award-covered. Read the consultation and redundancy clauses. Awards generally include standard consultation obligations for major workplace change. Enterprise agreements must contain consultation terms and may include redundancy rules that differ from the NES. Some industry awards also have specific redundancy schemes that change when redundancy applies, who is covered, or what must be paid.

  • Does the instrument require written information about the change?
  • Does it require consultation with representatives or unions?
  • Does it contain redundancy pay rules different from the NES?
  • Does it include dispute resolution steps that could be triggered?

Manager coaching language

“Your role in the first meeting is to explain the proposed business change clearly and compassionately. It is not to argue the outcome, defend every detail, or tell the employee the decision is final.”

Practice task: readiness gate Imagine a manager wants to hold the first meeting today but only has a verbal business reason and no vacancy list. Decide whether you would let the meeting proceed. Write the missing items you would require first.
Quick check

Which item is most important to prepare before the first proposed redundancy meeting?

Apply this on the job

Speed is not the same as readiness. If the business reason is sound but the process materials are not ready, pause long enough to prepare the employee conversation properly.

Module 03 lesson

Initial Notification Meeting

The first meeting is the moment that sets the tone. The employee should understand the proposed change, why their position is at risk, what happens next, how they can provide feedback, and what support is available.

What to say “These changes place your position at risk of redundancy. No final decision has been made. We want to give you time to consider the information and provide feedback.”
What not to say “Your role is redundant,” “There is nothing you can do,” “This is because performance has not been strong,” or “We have already decided.”

Meeting flow

  1. Open calmly and explain who is present.
  2. Read or summarise the proposed changes letter.
  3. Explain the business context and why the role is at risk.
  4. Confirm the decision is not final and invite feedback.
  5. Explain the consultation meeting date, support person option, and how to submit questions.
  6. Discuss immediate arrangements such as work attendance, systems, confidentiality, EAP, and safe travel.

Handling employee reactions

  • Shock: Pause, repeat the next step, and do not overload them with detail.
  • Anger: Acknowledge the impact and bring the conversation back to process.
  • Questions: Clarify what you can, then capture detailed questions for consultation.
  • No response: Give them time and confirm the next meeting in writing.
Practice task: first five minutes Draft the first five sentences you would ask the manager to say in the meeting. Your draft must include proposed-change language, the role at risk, no final decision, the next consultation meeting, and the support person option.
Quick check

Which sentence is best for the first meeting?

Apply this on the job

Keep the tone steady and the message short. Over-talking is common when managers feel uncomfortable; a clear pause is often more respectful than filling the room.

Module 04 lesson

Consultation and Redeployment

Consultation is more than telling. It is the stage where the employee can ask questions, challenge assumptions, suggest alternatives, and discuss whether redeployment is possible. The decision-maker must genuinely consider what is raised.

Consultation should cover The proposed change, expected impact, employee questions, alternatives, ways to reduce adverse effects, and current or potential redeployment options.
Redeployment should consider Current vacancies, suitable alternative roles, associated entities where relevant, location, skills, pay, training, and the employee's own preferences.

Consultation meeting prompts

  • “What questions do you have about the proposed change?”
  • “Is there anything you think we should consider before a final decision is made?”
  • “Are there alternative arrangements you would like us to assess?”
  • “Would you like to discuss any current vacancies or possible redeployment options?”
  • “Is there anything about the expected impact on you that we should understand?”

Decision-maker review

After consultation, HR should ensure the decision-maker receives the employee's feedback, any notes, and a clear recommendation or risk summary. If the employee has raised a practical alternative, it should be assessed and documented before the outcome is confirmed.

Practice task: feedback triage Sort employee feedback into four categories: clarify now, investigate before deciding, escalate for advice, or record as considered but not accepted. Then draft a short note to the decision-maker.
Quick check

The employee suggests reducing hours instead of removing the role. What should HR do next?

Apply this on the job

Do not just write “feedback noted.” Record what was raised, who considered it, and why it did or did not change the proposed outcome.

Module 05 lesson

Fair Selection and Risk Controls

Selection becomes important where the business needs fewer roles than the number of people currently performing similar work. HR's task is to keep the process relevant, objective, transparent, and free from discriminatory factors.

Lower-risk criteria Required skills, qualifications, role knowledge, objectively evidenced performance, capability against the future structure, and relevant experience.
Higher-risk criteria Attendance without context, age-linked tenure rules, subjective manager preference, untested assumptions, or criteria that map onto protected attributes.

Selection design steps

  1. Define the future role or smaller team requirement.
  2. Select criteria that directly relate to that requirement.
  3. Decide what evidence will be used for each criterion.
  4. Check for discrimination or adverse impact risks.
  5. Use more than one assessor where appropriate.
  6. Keep scoring notes factual, respectful, and reviewable.

Worked example

Instead of “least committed,” use “availability and capability to perform the required future operating model,” then define the evidence. Instead of “most sick leave,” consider whether attendance is truly inherent to the future role and seek advice before using it.

Using the matrix fairly

A matrix is not a magic answer. It is a way to make reasoning visible. Use it only after the future role requirements are clear. Scores should be supported by evidence, reviewed for consistency, and checked for discrimination risk. If two employees are close in score, look carefully at the evidence quality before treating the numbers as decisive.

Practice task: criteria repair Rewrite these flawed criteria: “least committed,” “most sick leave,” “most recent hire,” and “manager preference.” Replace each with a role-relevant criterion or flag it as too risky to use.
Quick check

Which selection criterion is usually the most defensible?

Apply this on the job

Write selection notes as though they may later be read by the employee, a regulator, or a tribunal. That discipline usually improves fairness and clarity fast.

Module 06 lesson

Final Decision, Entitlements, and Close-Out

The final stage should confirm the outcome clearly, but only after consultation and redeployment have been properly considered. HR then supports notice, final pay, systems access, equipment, team communications, wellbeing, and records.

Before confirming Check that consultation occurred, feedback was considered, redeployment was reviewed, decision-maker notes are complete, and entitlements have been checked.
After confirming Provide written notice, final pay details, support options, return of property steps, reference position if approved, and clear contact points.

Final meeting flow

  1. Thank the employee for engaging in consultation.
  2. Summarise what was considered, including feedback or alternatives.
  3. Confirm whether the redundancy is proceeding.
  4. Provide the written notice and explain key dates.
  5. Explain estimated payments, notice, leave, and any outplacement support.
  6. Confirm practical next steps and support after the meeting.

Entitlement checks

Fair Work guidance says redundancy pay may depend on continuous service, whether the employer has at least 15 employees, and whether an award or enterprise agreement changes the entitlement. Some employees, including casuals and employees with less than 12 months' service, may not receive redundancy pay under the NES. Always check the current facts and instruments before confirming figures.

When estimating redundancy pay, start with eligibility, then service, then base pay for ordinary hours. Redundancy pay is separate from notice, payment in lieu of notice, unused annual leave, long service leave, and any other final pay items.

Practice task: close-out file review Before confirming an outcome, check whether the file contains the proposed change letter, consultation notes, employee feedback, decision-maker review, redeployment evidence, payment estimate, written notice, and final communication plan.
Quick check

When is it appropriate to confirm the final redundancy outcome?

Apply this on the job

The employee does not need to agree with the outcome for the process to conclude. The organisation does need to show the process was genuine, considered, and properly documented.

Practice Toolkit

Use these tools as you work through the course. They are training aids, not approved company templates, and should be checked against policy before live use.

Email and Meeting templates for clients

Use these as starting points when helping a client or manager prepare written communications. Each template includes a short note on when to use it.

Initial Meeting Opening

Use this at the start of the first meeting to explain that the role is at risk and the change is still proposed.

We are meeting today to talk with you about proposed changes to the business. Unfortunately, these proposed changes mean your position of [job title] is at risk of redundancy. No final decision has been made. We want to provide you with the information today, give you time to consider it, and then meet again so you can ask questions and provide feedback before any final decision is made.
Consultation Meeting Invitation

Use this after the initial meeting to confirm the consultation meeting and support person option.

Following our meeting today, we would like to invite you to a consultation meeting on [date] at [time]. The purpose of the meeting is to discuss the proposed changes, answer any questions you may have, consider any feedback or alternatives you would like to raise, and discuss any current roles that may be suitable for redeployment. You may bring a support person if you wish.
Redeployment Discussion

Use this in consultation when discussing current vacancies or possible alternative roles.

As part of the consultation process, we also need to consider whether there are any suitable redeployment options. We have reviewed current vacancies and would like to discuss these with you. Please let us know if there are any roles you would like us to consider, including roles that may require training, different duties, different hours, or a different location.
Decision-Maker Feedback Note

Use this internally to summarise employee feedback for the person making the final decision.

Employee feedback received: [summarise feedback]. Questions raised: [summarise questions]. Alternatives suggested: [summarise alternatives]. Redeployment matters raised: [summarise]. Recommended consideration required: [what the decision-maker must consider]. Proposed response: [accept / investigate / not accepted, with reasons]. Decision-maker review completed by: [name/date].
Final Outcome Meeting Opening

Use this when consultation is complete and the final decision is being communicated.

Thank you for meeting with us and for providing your feedback during consultation. We have considered the feedback and questions you raised, including [briefly identify key matters]. After considering those matters and the available redeployment options, the business has decided that the redundancy will proceed. We will now talk through the written notice, key dates, payments, and support available to you.
When Feedback Changes the Outcome

Use this when consultation feedback means the business needs to pause or review the proposed change.

Thank you for the feedback and alternatives you provided during consultation. After considering the matters you raised, the business has decided to review the proposed change before making any final decision. We will come back to you by [date] with an update on next steps. No final redundancy decision has been made at this stage.

Pre-notification checklist
  • Business reason for proposed change is documented.
  • Future structure or operating model is clear enough to explain.
  • Affected role or roles are identified.
  • Applicable award, enterprise agreement, contract, and internal policy have been checked.
  • Decision-maker is identified and available to consider feedback.
  • Proposed changes letter and meeting script are prepared.
  • Current vacancies and possible redeployment options have been reviewed.
  • Redundancy pay estimate and notice assumptions have been checked by payroll or the responsible team.
  • Meeting time, privacy, note taker, employee support, and safe travel home have been planned.
Initial meeting wording guide

Use calm, direct, proposed-change language. For example:

“We are meeting today to talk with you about proposed changes to the business. Unfortunately, these proposed changes mean your position of [job title] is at risk of redundancy. No final decision has been made. We want to give you the letter today, allow you time to read it, and then meet again so you can ask questions and provide feedback before a final decision is made.”

Close the meeting by confirming the consultation meeting time, the support person option, how to submit questions, and any immediate practical arrangements.

Consultation question bank
  • What questions do you have about the proposed change?
  • Is there anything you believe we should consider before a final decision is made?
  • Are there alternatives to redundancy you would like us to assess?
  • Are there current vacancies you would like to discuss as potential redeployment options?
  • Is there anything about the impact on you that we should understand?
  • Would you like time to provide further written feedback after this meeting?
Selection criteria risk check

Before using selection criteria, ask:

  • Is this criterion directly relevant to the future role or operating model?
  • Can we evidence it objectively?
  • Would it unfairly disadvantage someone because of a protected attribute or protected absence?
  • Would the employee understand why this criterion was used?
  • Would the notes still read as fair and respectful if reviewed externally?
Final decision file check
  • Proposed change letter was issued.
  • Consultation meeting notes are complete.
  • Employee feedback and questions were captured.
  • Decision-maker considered the feedback before the outcome was confirmed.
  • Redeployment options were reviewed and documented.
  • Entitlements, notice, and final pay were checked.
  • Written notice of redundancy was provided if the decision proceeded.
  • Exit steps, equipment, systems access, EAP, outplacement, and internal communications were completed.

Key Takeaways

These are the messages you should be able to apply without notes after the course.

Role, not person Redundancy should be anchored to operational change and the future need for the job.
Proposed means proposed The employee must have a real chance to provide feedback before final confirmation.
Redeployment matters Open roles and associated entities should be considered where relevant.
Selection must be fair Criteria should be objective, transparent, relevant, and non-discriminatory.
Records protect the process Document business reasons, feedback, decision-maker review, and final outcomes.
Check entitlements carefully Pay and notice depend on facts, instruments, and policy.
