Redundancy Basics

Before following the process, understand what redundancy is, why the process matters, and where the main risks sit.

What redundancy means

Redundancy is about the job no longer needing to be done by anyone. Common reasons include restructure, lower work volumes, technology changes, relocation, closure, merger, takeover, insolvency, or bankruptcy.

What makes it genuine

The job is no longer required, consultation obligations in an award or enterprise agreement have been followed, and reasonable redeployment was not available.

Why process matters

If the process is not genuine, the employee may be able to challenge the dismissal. The biggest risks are pre-determining the outcome, failing to consult, ignoring redeployment, or using redundancy to mask performance issues.

What awards usually say

Awards contain consultation clauses. They generally require employers to notify affected employees, provide information, invite views, and consider those views before implementing major workplace change.

What agreements may say

Enterprise agreements must contain consultation terms. They may also include redundancy rules or entitlements that differ from the NES, so always check the agreement itself.

What HR must protect

Fairness, consistency, documentation, employee dignity, confidentiality, decision-maker independence, and evidence that feedback was considered before the final decision.

Redundancy Process Map

This is the meeting and decision flow students should understand before using the module lessons. The key idea: notify proposed change, consult, consider, then confirm.

1 Business case

Document why the role or number of roles is proposed to change. Confirm the job is genuinely no longer required in the future structure.

2 Pre-checks

Check award, agreement, contract, policy, selection needs, redeployment options, decision-maker, payroll data, and consultation obligations.

3 Initial meeting

Notify the employee of proposed changes and that their position is at risk. Provide written information and confirm the consultation meeting.

4 Consultation

Invite questions and feedback, discuss impacts and alternatives, review vacancies, and capture the employee's response.

5 Consider feedback

The decision-maker reviews feedback, redeployment, and alternatives. Do not treat feedback as merely “noted.”

6 Final outcome

Confirm the decision in writing, explain payments and notice, offer support, complete exit steps, and keep the process file.

Timing guide: internal materials suggest consultation is ideally 1-2 weeks where practical, but timing depends on the circumstances, award/agreement requirements, business urgency, and whether the employee raises feedback that needs proper consideration.

Who may not get redundancy pay under the NES?

Important distinction: “No redundancy pay” does not always mean “no redundancy process.” First ask whether redundancy pay is owed. Separately ask whether consultation, selection, redeployment, notice, final pay, contract, policy, discrimination, general protections, or unfair dismissal risk still needs to be managed.
Question 1: Is redundancy pay owed?

Check employer size, service length, employee type, award/agreement terms, industry schemes, and NES exclusions.

Question 2: Is a process still needed?

Check award/agreement consultation, company policy, redeployment, selection fairness, notice, final pay, and protected reason risks.

Question 3: Can the process be lighter?

Sometimes yes, especially small business plus under 12 months, but only after checking contract, policy, award/agreement, notice, and general protections.

Less than 12 months

Employees with less than 12 months continuous service generally do not receive NES redundancy pay. In a larger business, still follow the redundancy consultation process where an award, enterprise agreement, policy, or risk profile requires it.

Casual employees

Casual employees are listed by Fair Work as employees who do not get redundancy pay.

Fixed period, project, or season

Employees engaged for a specific period, task, project, or season may be excluded.

Apprentices and some trainees

Apprentices and trainees engaged only for the training arrangement may not get redundancy pay.

Serious misconduct

Employees terminated for serious misconduct do not receive redundancy pay.

Small business employer

Most small businesses with fewer than 15 employees do not pay redundancy pay under the NES, but exceptions and industry schemes can apply. Still check notice, final pay, contract, policy, award/agreement, and protected reason risks.

Pay and process do not always line up

Scenario Redundancy pay position Process position Practical caution
Large business, employee has less than 12 months service Generally no NES redundancy pay. Usually still run a clear redundancy process, especially award/agreement consultation, policy, redeployment, and documentation. Do not treat “no pay” as “no process.” Notice and final pay still need to be checked.
Small business, employee has less than 12 months service Generally no NES redundancy pay. A full redundancy consultation process may not be required if no award/agreement/policy applies, but still check notice, final pay, contract, discrimination, general protections, and whether the stated business reason is supportable. Do not confuse unfair dismissal eligibility with redundancy pay entitlement.

Selection Matrix Template

When there are multiple employees in the same or very similar role, selection must be fair, relevant, objective, and documented. A skills and competency matrix helps keep the assessment focused on future role requirements rather than preference.

How to use this template: define the future role requirements first, choose criteria that directly connect to those requirements, set evidence sources, score consistently, and have the decision reviewed before communicating outcomes. Do not use protected attributes, unsupported assumptions, or criteria that indirectly discriminate.

Redundancy Toolkit

Use these tools when preparing redundancy advice, client communications, file notes, and process checks. They are training aids, not approved company templates, and should be checked against policy before live use.

Email and Meeting templates for clients

Use these as starting points when helping a client or manager prepare written communications. Each template includes a short note on when to use it.

Initial Meeting Opening

Use this at the start of the first meeting to explain that the role is at risk and the change is still proposed.

We are meeting today to talk with you about proposed changes to the business. Unfortunately, these proposed changes mean your position of [job title] is at risk of redundancy. No final decision has been made. We want to provide you with the information today, give you time to consider it, and then meet again so you can ask questions and provide feedback before any final decision is made.
Consultation Meeting Invitation

Use this after the initial meeting to confirm the consultation meeting and support person option.

Following our meeting today, we would like to invite you to a consultation meeting on [date] at [time]. The purpose of the meeting is to discuss the proposed changes, answer any questions you may have, consider any feedback or alternatives you would like to raise, and discuss any current roles that may be suitable for redeployment. You may bring a support person if you wish.
Redeployment Discussion

Use this in consultation when discussing current vacancies or possible alternative roles.

As part of the consultation process, we also need to consider whether there are any suitable redeployment options. We have reviewed current vacancies and would like to discuss these with you. Please let us know if there are any roles you would like us to consider, including roles that may require training, different duties, different hours, or a different location.
Decision-Maker Feedback Note

Use this internally to summarise employee feedback for the person making the final decision.

Employee feedback received: [summarise feedback]. Questions raised: [summarise questions]. Alternatives suggested: [summarise alternatives]. Redeployment matters raised: [summarise]. Recommended consideration required: [what the decision-maker must consider]. Proposed response: [accept / investigate / not accepted, with reasons]. Decision-maker review completed by: [name/date].
Final Outcome Meeting Opening

Use this when consultation is complete and the final decision is being communicated.

Thank you for meeting with us and for providing your feedback during consultation. We have considered the feedback and questions you raised, including [briefly identify key matters]. After considering those matters and the available redeployment options, the business has decided that the redundancy will proceed. We will now talk through the written notice, key dates, payments, and support available to you.
When Feedback Changes the Outcome

Use this when consultation feedback means the business needs to pause or review the proposed change.

Thank you for the feedback and alternatives you provided during consultation. After considering the matters you raised, the business has decided to review the proposed change before making any final decision. We will come back to you by [date] with an update on next steps. No final redundancy decision has been made at this stage.

Checklists

Use these checklists to prepare meetings, test process risk, and review the file before a final outcome is confirmed.

Pre-notification checklist
  • Business reason for proposed change is documented.
  • Future structure or operating model is clear enough to explain.
  • Affected role or roles are identified.
  • Applicable award, enterprise agreement, contract, and internal policy have been checked.
  • Decision-maker is identified and available to consider feedback.
  • Proposed changes letter and meeting script are prepared.
  • Current vacancies and possible redeployment options have been reviewed.
  • Redundancy pay estimate and notice assumptions have been checked by payroll or the responsible team.
  • Meeting time, privacy, note taker, employee support, and safe travel home have been planned.
Consultation question bank
  • What questions do you have about the proposed change?
  • Is there anything you believe we should consider before a final decision is made?
  • Are there alternatives to redundancy you would like us to assess?
  • Are there current vacancies you would like to discuss as potential redeployment options?
  • Is there anything about the impact on you that we should understand?
  • Would you like time to provide further written feedback after this meeting?
Selection criteria risk check

Before using selection criteria, ask:

  • Is this criterion directly relevant to the future role or operating model?
  • Can we evidence it objectively?
  • Would it unfairly disadvantage someone because of a protected attribute or protected absence?
  • Would the employee understand why this criterion was used?
  • Would the notes still read as fair and respectful if reviewed externally?
Final decision file check
  • Proposed change letter was issued.
  • Consultation meeting notes are complete.
  • Employee feedback and questions were captured.
  • Decision-maker considered the feedback before the outcome was confirmed.
  • Redeployment options were reviewed and documented.
  • Entitlements, notice, and final pay were checked.
  • Written notice of redundancy was provided if the decision proceeded.
  • Exit steps, equipment, systems access, EAP, outplacement, and internal communications were completed.
